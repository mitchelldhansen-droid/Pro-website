The Time of the Indie Dev

Jul 14, 2026 · 4 min read

In November 2023, I was an Associate Producer at Digital Extremes. We had just shipped Wayfinder - 750,000+ lifetime copies across PC and Console, and within the same month I was part of the restructuring wave that hit almost everyone I know in this industry. Over 25,000 game developers lost their jobs across 2023 and 2024, and the bleeding hasn't stopped - roughly 50,000 gone since 2022. I've spent the time since pitching publishers, co-founding an indie studio, and teaching myself to write the code I used to schedule.

So when I say this is the best time in history to be a small game team, understand that I'm not saying it from the sidelines. I'm saying it from inside the wreckage, where the view is surprisingly good.

Why AAA broke first

The math stopped working. Jason Schreier puts a typical AAA budget at $300 million or more these days, with roughly 6 million copies sold as the break-even point. Six million copies at $70 just to get back to zero. That's not a business model, that's a coin flip with a decade of people's careers on it. And so AAA has no choice but to ride the IPs people already know — Call of Duty, Grand Theft Auto, The Elder Scrolls - and even then, the games sometimes don't perform the way projections said they should. When the bet does miss, it misses catastrophically: Concord reportedly cost north of $200 million, launched to 697 peak concurrent players on Steam, and was shut down within two weeks. The studio closed with it. Tango Gameworks shipped an award-winning critical darling in Hi-Fi Rush and got closed anyway. Shipping something great is no longer a protection against the onslaught of “internal restructuring”.

Part of how we got here: games had an investment boom during Covid, and people spent like the money and the runway would never run out. But you can't treat a creative and technical endeavor like simple software.

You can't treat something that relies on fun like a piggy bank.

Now run the indie math

A $20 indie needs roughly 50,000 copies to recoup a modest budget. That's the whole bar. The developer of Balatro - one person - was stunned when he crossed 50,000 sales. The game went on to sell more than 5 million copies in under a year. Megabonk, another solo developer, sold a million copies in two weeks this past fall. Ball x Pit hit a million in about six weeks. Slay the Spire II sold 3 million copies in its first week of Early Access this March - rebuilt in Godot after the Unity fallout a few years back, the same free engine I work in every day. Behind them stands a decade of proof: Valheim, Hades, Among Us, Slay the Spire.

Selling 100K copies at that scale is a real success. 500K is amazing. A million is a dream that now comes true multiple times a year. And every one of those outcomes leaves money and runway for the next game - instead of spending $200 million, recouping it if you're lucky, and having no capital left to build what comes next.

The player math points in the same direction. Why would someone spend $70 on one game when the same money buys three or four indie titles - often with more hours and more joy in them? Players have already voted. Look at any Steam top-seller list from the past two years and count the small teams.

What it demands from small teams

Here's the part I care about most, because it's the part I live: none of this means small teams get to be sloppy. The opposite. When you're six people, there is no department to absorb a missed dependency. Every hour of rework costs a meaningful percentage of your total capacity. The indie teams winning right now aren't winning because they skipped process - they're winning because they run tight, lightweight versions of the discipline AAA does at scale: real milestones, real scope conversations, real willingness to say "this won't fit" before it's a crisis. On my current projects I run sprint planning and async standups across timezones, and we delivered our last vertical slice about twelve days ahead of schedule. The craft of production doesn't disappear at small scale. It concentrates.

I'm not anti-AAA. Shipping one was the education of my life, and the people still inside those teams are some of the best I've worked with and still great friends. But the center of gravity for creative risk has moved. It now lives with teams of one, two, six - people pushing on what a game can even be, because their break-even point makes the push affordable.

That's where I've planted my flag. I'm co-developing a 3D cozy builder and platformer, and just wrapped a vertical slice as producer and engineering lead on a visual novel/turn-based RPG. I'll be writing more about both - the systems, the schedules, and what a AAA process looks like when you shrink it to fit 6, 4 or even 2 people.

It's the time of the indie dev. Come watch me build something with big intentions.
